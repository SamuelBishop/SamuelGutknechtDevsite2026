# Design Brief

## Direction

Create a personal portfolio that feels professional, warm, and lived-in. It should communicate senior engineering judgment without presenting Samuel as a brand campaign. The experience should be calm, direct, and easy to scan, with enough personality to feel like a person’s home on the web.

Use a subtle Pacific Northwest and outdoors influence through restrained natural color, photography, texture, or language. Avoid turning the site into an outdoor-themed template; professional work remains the focus.

## Desired qualities

- Clear, capable, and grounded
- Warm rather than corporate
- Personal rather than performative
- Curated rather than exhaustive
- Polished without feeling precious
- Quietly technical without resembling developer documentation

## Visual guidance

- Use clean, highly legible typography with a restrained type scale.
- Establish hierarchy through type, spacing, and composition before decoration.
- Favor generous whitespace and comfortable reading widths.
- Use a neutral foundation with one or two warm, nature-influenced accent colors.
- Let real work imagery and personal photography add character when available.
- Keep cards and surfaces simple; borders, shadows, and rounded corners should be subtle.
- Use motion sparingly for orientation or feedback, not spectacle.

## Do

- Make the main message understandable within a quick scan.
- Keep navigation familiar and consistent.
- Balance professional work with a small amount of personal context.
- Use concrete, plain language and short content blocks.
- Give each selected work item room to breathe.
- Make links, controls, and interactive states visually obvious.
- Respect reduced-motion preferences.

## Avoid

- A giant resume rendered as a homepage.
- Generic startup language, oversized claims, or sales-style calls to action.
- Skill meters, logo clouds, decorative dashboards, and technology-list overload.
- Excessive gradients, glass effects, shadows, animations, or parallax.
- Dense grids of nearly identical cards.
- Outdoor motifs that compete with content or feel costume-like.
- Claims, metrics, client details, or imagery that are confidential or unverified.

## Accessibility expectations

- Target WCAG 2.2 AA for color contrast and interaction patterns.
- Use semantic page structure and a logical heading order.
- Support keyboard-only navigation with visible focus states.
- Provide useful alternative text for meaningful images and empty alt text for decorative images.
- Do not rely on color, hover, or motion alone to convey information.
- Ensure controls have clear names and adequately sized touch targets.
- Keep text readable at browser zoom and with user font-size preferences.

## Responsive expectations

- Design mobile-first and support small phones through wide desktop screens.
- Preserve readable line lengths and hierarchy rather than merely shrinking the desktop layout.
- Allow navigation, cards, media, and resume content to reflow without horizontal scrolling.
- Keep primary actions easy to reach and tap on small screens.
- Test long titles, missing imagery, and variable content lengths.

## Motion expectations

- Prefer short, subtle transitions for navigation, focus, hover, and content state changes.
- Avoid blocking entrance sequences or movement tied aggressively to scrolling.
- Provide an equivalent, stable experience when reduced motion is enabled.
